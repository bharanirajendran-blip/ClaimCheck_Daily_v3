The claim that glyphosate weedkiller causes cancer is not clear-cut. While some politicians assert it causes cancer, the scientific evidence is inconsistent. Regulatory agencies like the EPA find it unlikely to be carcinogenic, whereas the IARC classifies it as probably carcinogenic. The distinction between occupational and trace exposure is crucial, with the latter showing little evidence of cancer risk.

Key Evidence
  • The EPA states glyphosate is 'unlikely to be a human carcinogen' when used according to label instructions.
  • The IARC classified glyphosate as 'probably carcinogenic to humans' based on animal studies and limited human evidence.
  • A large NIH study found no association between glyphosate exposure and non-Hodgkin lymphoma in agricultural workers.
  • FactCheck.org notes that there is little evidence suggesting glyphosate causes cancer in trace amounts present in food.

This refers to the first direct laboratory measurement of a proton capture reaction on arsenic-73, which is a significant scientific milestone. However, the reaction itself occurs naturally in supernovae and was theoretically known, making the headline slightly sensationalized.
