"This 67,800-year-old handprint is the oldest art ever found"

Source: Science Daily — Top Science

Confidence: 90%

The claim that a 67,800-year-old handprint is the oldest art ever found is mostly true. The hand stencil, found in a cave in Sulawesi, Indonesia, has been dated using uranium-series dating to at least 67,800 years ago, making it the oldest reliably dated cave art. However, the definition of 'art' can vary, and other symbolic artifacts might be older. The research is credible, conducted by a reputable international team, and uses established dating methods.

Key Evidence
  • The claim that a 67,800-year-old handprint is 'the oldest art ever found' appears largely accurate with important caveats. The source article from Science Daily reports on research led by credible institutions (Griffith University and Indonesia's BRIN), using established uranium-series dating methods—the same technique that has been used successfully to date other ancient cave art in the region.
  • The discovery builds on previous work by the same team, which identified progressively older cave art in Sulawesi over the past decade. The 2024 finding (previously the oldest) is now surpassed by at least 15,000 years. The research appears methodologically sound: uranium-series dating of calcite layers provides minimum ages, and the team examined layers both over and beneath the artwork for accuracy.
  • The claim applies to cave art/rock art. Other symbolic artifacts—such as the ~77,000-year-old ochre engravings from Blombos Cave, South Africa, or ~100,000+ year-old shell beads—might be considered 'art' by some definitions.
  • The hand stencil in Sulawesi, dated via uranium-series analysis to at least 67,800 years ago, is credibly the oldest reliably dated cave art discovered to date. The caveat is that 'oldest art' depends on how one defines art; other symbolic objects may be older.
